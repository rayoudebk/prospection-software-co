**you're a senior associate at JP Morgan working on TMT but for SMEs no large deals, whats your sourcing process to identify M&A targets when you work on the buy-side? what are the different steps, what is the mental model you use, framework, actions you take (eg. crawl target A website), tools you use or databases, what information is helpful for the ranking of targets to present to the VP, what are the information that are the most helpful to identify correct targets (either horizontal or vertical M&A)? please describe thoroughly for another senior associate working at BNP Paribas who's familiar with it.**


For SME TMT buy-side, the sourcing process is not “find good companies.” It is “reduce a very large, noisy universe into a short list of companies that are both strategically relevant and realistically actionable.” Those are two different problems. A lot of juniors are decent at the first and weak at the second.

The mental model I use is simple:

Start with the acquirer’s deal logic. Translate that into a target pattern. Build a universe that approximates that pattern. Enrich each company with evidence. Rank on strategic fit, quality, and executability. Then present only the names where the likely answer to “why this company, why now, why us” is credible.

For SMEs, especially in TMT, you are usually dealing with imperfect data, fragmented private markets, inconsistent financial disclosure, fuzzy product descriptions, and lots of businesses that sound similar but are structurally very different. So the process has to be evidence-based and iterative.

The core framework

I usually think in five layers.

Layer 1 is mandate framing. What are we actually trying to buy?
Layer 2 is market map. What does the relevant sub-sector look like?
Layer 3 is company identification. Who exists in that space?
Layer 4 is target scoring. Which names are attractive enough to spend time on?
Layer 5 is execution filtering. Which names are actually contactable, sellable, and financeable?

If you skip Layer 1 and Layer 5, you get beautiful longlists that never convert into processes.

1. Mandate framing: define the acquisition logic first

Before I pull any company names, I want the acquisition logic to be painfully explicit. Otherwise every software company starts to look “interesting.”

For a buy-side SME TMT mandate, I frame the thesis around a few questions.

What is the client trying to buy: revenue growth, product capability, customer access, geography, talent, margin improvement, or defensibility?

Is the deal horizontal, vertical, or adjacency?

Horizontal means buying a similar business to consolidate share, customers, or product breadth. Typical logic is scale, cross-sell, geography, or reducing fragmentation.

Vertical means moving up or down the value chain. Typical logic is owning more of the workflow, improving margins, securing supply, improving switching costs, or getting closer to the end customer.

Adjacency means not directly same category, but same customer base or workflow neighborhood. This is where a lot of TMT deals happen, but this is also where bad sourcing happens because adjacency gets defined too loosely.

For the mandate, I want at least these inputs from the client or sponsor:

Business description in plain English, not just SIC/NAICS
Current products/modules
Customer segments
Sales motion: SMB, mid-market, enterprise, channel, embedded, OEM
Geography
Deal rationale
Revenue and EBITDA range
Desired business model: software, services-heavy software, data, infra, marketplace, managed services
Minimum recurring revenue quality
Tolerance for services mix
Tolerance for customer concentration
Desired ownership outcome: majority, full buyout, carve-out, minority with path, founder rollover
Known red flags they do not want: regulated exposure, hardware, low gross margin, project-based revenue, public sector dependency, etc.

Then I convert this into a target pattern. For example:

“French/Benelux B2B software vendors selling workflow or compliance tools into banks, insurers, wealth managers, or brokers; €3–15m revenue; sticky recurring revenue; founder-owned or lightly institutionalized; not pure consulting; evidence of product IP; manageable services component.”

That is already much more useful than “fintech software targets.”

2. Build the market map: define where targets can live

This step matters because most bad longlists are just keyword dumps from databases. Good sourcing comes from a market map first.

I map the space along four axes.

First, customer. Who buys? Banks, insurers, wealth managers, brokers, PSPs, CFOs, hospitals, retailers, industrials, etc.

Second, use case. What workflow is being solved? KYC, treasury, portfolio accounting, invoicing, cybersecurity posture, CRM, ERP connector layer, marketing analytics, payment orchestration, etc.

Third, product architecture. System of record, system of workflow, data layer, analytics layer, integration layer, tooling layer, outsourced managed service.

Fourth, monetization and delivery. License, SaaS, managed service, transaction fee, implementation-heavy, reseller-led, embedded, on-prem, hybrid.

This gives you a grid rather than a list. That matters because it lets you see white spaces and separates direct peers from adjacent names.

For horizontal M&A, I care a lot about same customer and same workflow, then product and GTM overlap.
For vertical M&A, I care more about same workflow chain and control points in the stack.
For adjacency, I care about shared buyer, shared data objects, and shared implementation context.

Example:

If the acquirer sells portfolio management software to private banks, horizontal targets are other PMS/OMS or reporting vendors. Vertical targets might be data reconciliation, custody workflow, compliance tooling, client reporting engines, payments rails, or onboarding/KYC vendors that touch the same operating chain.

That market map becomes the sourcing backbone.

How I actually build the first universe

I do it through multiple paths in parallel.

Path A: database screens
I use database filters to get an initial cut by geography, industry, revenue, ownership, employee count, keywords, funding status, and business description.

But I do not trust database industry tags too much in SME TMT. They are often wrong or too broad. So I use them as coarse filters only.

Path B: peer expansion
Start from a few known good companies. Then find peers, competitors, “alternatives,” integration partners, former management teams, customer references, and category pages. This is usually higher signal than pure screening.

Path C: customer-backward mapping
If the acquirer cares about a particular buyer segment, I map from the customer side: what software vendors serve this workflow? I use customer case studies, implementation partners, app marketplaces, conference sponsors, procurement lists, and job descriptions mentioning tools.

Path D: workflow/value-chain mapping
Especially for vertical M&A, I break the workflow into steps and identify who owns each step. Then I identify product vendors at each step.

Example:
origination → onboarding/KYC → pricing → execution → reconciliation → reporting → billing → treasury → analytics

That often surfaces targets databases miss because the product description doesn’t use the keyword you searched.

Path E: keyword and website crawling
In fragmented niches, I search websites for specific language patterns, vertical terms, compliance terms, integration terms, buyer terms, and product phrases. For example, “core banking integration,” “MiFID reporting,” “merchant onboarding API,” “SEPA reconciliation,” “portfolio accounting,” etc. This is often extremely effective in Europe.

Path F: transaction and sponsor trail
Look at who has been bought, who was considered in similar past processes, which founders were approached before, and what sponsor theses exist in the sector. That helps anchor both valuation reality and buyer appetite.

4. First-pass filtering: remove obvious non-targets early

Before deep enrichment, I kill obvious false positives fast. Otherwise you waste time.

The first-pass filters are usually:

Not enough product/IP, too services-led
Wrong geography or too hard to buy
Too small to matter or too big for mandate
Wrong customer base
Too much hardware/resale revenue
No recurring component
Public sector dependency if client dislikes that
Poor strategic adjacency
Clear legal/regulatory complexity
Too venture-priced for an SME buyer
Subsidiary of large group with no carve-out logic
Zombie businesses with no growth, no product investment, no defensibility

At this stage, I care more about disqualifying efficiently than scoring precisely.

6. What information is most helpful to identify the right targets

This is the part many people get wrong. The best indicators are not always formal financials, especially before management contact.

The highest-signal information tends to be:

First, customer-workflow fit. Does the company solve a workflow that matters to the acquirer’s customers? This is more important than broad sector labels.

Second, product specificity. Can you tell what the product actually does? A precise product and clear use case beats a vague “digital transformation” business every time.

Third, evidence of recurring value. Recurring contracts, embedded workflows, integrations, regulatory or operational criticality, switching costs.

Fourth, customer base overlap or adjacency. Shared buyers are extremely powerful because they imply cross-sell or GTM leverage.

Fifth, ownership/sellability. Is there a plausible reason this business could transact in the next 12–24 months?

Sixth, implementation model. Heavy implementation and customization can destroy scalability, but some services component is acceptable if it drives lock-in and margins are still healthy.

Seventh, proof of market relevance. Case studies, logos, partner ecosystem, hiring, certifications, conference footprint, founder visibility.

Eighth, valuation realism. A beautiful business with venture-style expectations may still be a poor buy-side target for an SME client.

If I had to choose the single most useful thing, it is not “revenue size.” It is a well-evidenced answer to: what part of the customer workflow do they own, and why does that matter strategically to our client?

7. Horizontal vs vertical M&A: what changes in the sourcing logic

Horizontal M&A

Here the logic is usually scale, market consolidation, geographic expansion, product breadth, and cost/operating leverage.

What I prioritize:
Same or very similar buyer
Same workflow or adjacent feature set
Comparable pricing model
Geographic complementarity
Cross-sell potential
Customer overlap risk
Ability to rationalize duplicate functions
Brand strength in a subsegment
Installed base
Retention / mission-criticality

What matters most:
Real product comparability
Commercial footprint
Integration ease
Synergy credibility
Competitive position

Key traps:
Thinking every competitor is buyable
Ignoring customer overlap dis-synergies
Overpaying for “growth” that is actually services
Buying weak products just because they are in the same category

Vertical M&A

Here the logic is different. It is about owning more of the stack, improving margin, securing capability, or deepening control of the workflow.

What I prioritize:
Position in the value chain
Control point in data or transactions
Ability to bundle into current offering
Margin transfer opportunity
Reduced dependency on third parties
Implementation leverage
Shared customer workflow
Operational interdependence
Potential to improve retention or switching costs

What matters most:
Workflow adjacency
Technical/API compatibility
Commercial bundling logic
Ability to remove frictions or capture more wallet share

Key traps:
Calling something vertical when it is just random adjacency
Buying something upstream/downstream that the client cannot actually integrate commercially
Underestimating operational complexity
Buying capability that customers do not value enough to pay for

8. Ranking: how I score targets before presenting upward

I typically do not use a giant fake-precision model. I use a practical scorecard with weighted dimensions, usually on a 1–5 scale. The point is not mathematics. The point is forcing comparability and discipline.

A typical scorecard:

Strategic fit: 25%
How directly does it map to the mandate?

Customer/workflow overlap: 15%
Do they serve the same buyer or same critical workflow?

Product quality and defensibility: 15%
Is there evidence of real IP and stickiness?

Financial attractiveness: 15%
Scale, growth, margin, recurring quality, likely valuation sanity.

Transaction feasibility: 15%
Ownership, sellability, founder stage, process likelihood.

Synergy potential: 10%
Revenue, product, geography, cost, stack rationalization.

Risk/complexity: 5%
Execution difficulty, integration burden, regulatory issues, customer concentration.

For a sponsor client, I may weight financial quality and exitability more heavily.
For a corporate client, I may weight strategic fit and workflow adjacency more heavily.

I also bucket names into:

Tier 1: immediate outreach candidates
Tier 2: strategically relevant but needs more work
Tier 3: keep in database/watchlist
Tier 4: not a priority / reject

That forces a recommendation rather than a research dump.

9. What I would actually do at the desk: concrete actions

If you want the workflow in practical sequence, this is roughly how I’d run it.

Step 1
Take client brief and rewrite it as a sourcing hypothesis with explicit inclusion/exclusion criteria.

Step 2
Map the relevant sub-sector and value chain. Define where horizontal peers and vertical extensions sit.

Step 3
Seed the universe with known names: competitors, partners, adjacent vendors, prior deal comps, sponsor portfolios.

Step 4
Run database screens to broaden the list by geography, size, sector, ownership, keywords.

Step 5
Manually crawl websites of the best seed names and extract high-signal descriptors:
buyer type, workflow, modules, industry language, customer logos, integrations, delivery model.

Step 6
Use those descriptors to search for more companies through Google, databases, partner directories, conference lists, app marketplaces, and review sites.

Step 7
Create a longlist with standardized fields. De-duplicate aggressively.

Step 8
Remove obvious non-targets quickly based on fit, business model, and feasibility.

Step 9
Enrich the remaining names with evidence:
website, LinkedIn, filings, ownership clues, employee count, product clues, customer proof, likely sellability.

Step 10
Score each target against the mandate. Force a view on why it matters.

Step 11
For top names, write 3–5 lines of investment logic and 2–3 lines of key concerns. If you cannot do that, the company is not ready.

Step 12
Present a short list to the VP with a recommendation framework, not just a list of companies.

11. What the VP actually wants to see

The VP does not want 120 names with superficial descriptions. The VP wants a point of view.

A good output for the VP is usually:

A one-page market map
A prioritized short list
For each top target:
what they do,
why they fit,
how they compare to others,
what we know about ownership and feasibility,
what the likely issues are,
and what next action is recommended

The useful output is not “here are 50 French fintech software companies.”
The useful output is:
“Here are 12 credible targets, 5 horizontal and 7 vertical. Of those, 4 look immediately actionable because the strategic fit is strong and ownership seems realistic. These 3 are the best because they solve adjacent workflows for the same buyer base and appear sticky, founder-owned, and subscale enough to transact.”

12. What separates strong sourcing from weak sourcing

Weak sourcing is database-led, taxonomy-blind, and detached from actual buyer workflow.

Strong sourcing is:
mandate-specific,
workflow-specific,
evidence-backed,
explicit on sellability,
and opinionated on prioritization.

In low/mid-market TMT, the highest edge often comes from doing the manual work better than others:
reading product pages carefully,
understanding who actually buys the product,
seeing through fake SaaS,
mapping the workflow properly,
and identifying owner situations before a process starts.

That is why “crawl target A website” is not a minor action. It is often one of the highest-value actions. A website tells you far more than a database tag: who the buyer is, what the workflow is, whether the product is real, whether the team has focus, whether the value proposition is horizontal or vertical, and whether this is software or consulting wearing a software costume.

For each company, I would want clear answers to these questions:

What exact problem do they solve?
For whom?
Where do they sit in the workflow?
Why does that matter to our client?
Is this really software/IP or mostly services?
How sticky is it?
How scalable is it?
How likely are they to sell?
What could go wrong in diligence?
Why should this be in the top decile of our longlist?

If you cannot answer those, you do not yet know the target.

14. If I were coaching another senior associate at BNP

I would say this:

Do not start from the database. Start from the acquirer’s workflow and M&A logic.

Do not confuse category overlap with strategic fit. “Fintech” or “martech” is uselessly broad.

Do not over-rely on revenue estimates early. Product/workflow fit and sellability are more predictive for prioritization.

Do not build giant undifferentiated longlists. Build a thesis-backed universe with explicit reasons for inclusion.

Do not present names without a view on ownership and actionability.

And most importantly: force every target into one sentence that explains why it belongs on the list. If that sentence sounds generic, the target probably is.

**would this be similar for a private equity senior associate and a corporate development VP? what would be common, and what would be different?**

Broadly yes, the sourcing machinery is similar. The pipeline is still:

define mandate,
build market map,
generate universe,
enrich targets,
rank,
then push into outreach or deeper work.

But the center of gravity is different.

A PE senior associate is usually asking, “Is this a good company I can buy at a workable price and improve or exit well?” A corp dev VP is usually asking, “Is this the right asset for this company’s strategy, and can we integrate it without breaking anything?” Same process, different objective function.

What is common

Both care about strategic fit first. Nobody serious runs sourcing as a blind screen on revenue and EBITDA. Both need a clear investment or acquisition thesis, otherwise the universe becomes noise.

Both will build a market map. They still need to understand the value chain, customer workflows, competitor set, adjacent categories, and where the target sits.

Both will use the same raw inputs. Company websites, databases, filings, LinkedIn, customer references, product documentation, partner ecosystems, transaction comps, and management or ownership clues all matter to both.

Both will rank targets. Neither should just dump a longlist upward. They need a prioritized view with reasons, risks, and recommended next actions.

Both care about quality of business model. Revenue quality, stickiness, customer concentration, product depth, services intensity, ownership structure, and likely valuation all matter in both cases.

Both care about executability. A target can be strategically attractive and still not be worth pursuing because it will not sell, is too expensive, has governance issues, or is too hard to diligence.

Where they diverge most

The difference is not really in the sourcing steps. It is in what gets weighted most heavily inside the ranking.

For private equity, the core question is whether the company is a good standalone investment. For corporate development, the core question is whether the company is a good strategic fit inside the buyer.

That changes almost everything downstream.

How a PE senior associate thinks

PE is more underwriting-led.

The associate is usually more focused on whether the target can produce an attractive risk-adjusted return. That means heavier emphasis on:

quality of earnings,
margin structure,
cash conversion,
exit multiple potential,
resilience,
fragmentation / buy-and-build logic,
room for operational improvement,
management quality,
scalability,
and valuation discipline.

The PE person still cares about product and strategic positioning, especially in TMT, but mostly because those drive durability, growth, and exitability.

They also tend to think more in terms of archetypes:

platform candidate,
add-on,
proprietary angle,
broken-but-fixable,
subscale roll-up piece,
premium asset worth stretching for,
or asset to avoid because quality is fake.

A PE associate will often tolerate less-than-perfect strategic adjacency if the asset is financially attractive and can fit into a broader investment thesis. A corp dev team usually cannot.

How a corp dev VP thinks

Corp dev is integration- and strategy-led.

The VP is usually more focused on whether the asset improves the parent company’s position. That means heavier emphasis on:

customer overlap or adjacency,
workflow fit,
product complementarity,
cross-sell potential,
technical integration,
channel leverage,
geographic expansion relevance,
defensive logic,
time-to-value,
and internal organizational feasibility.

The corp dev VP is asking:
Will our sales team know how to sell this?
Will customers care about the combination?
Can product and engineering integrate it?
Will leadership actually support the roadmap?
Does it solve a gap that matters now?

A corp dev VP may accept a weaker standalone financial profile if the strategic logic is very strong. PE usually has less room for that unless it is part of a clear transformation or roll-up thesis.

Same target, different reactions

Take a niche compliance software vendor with €6m revenue, 85% recurring, moderate growth, and strong bank customers.

PE might say:
Good retention, sticky product, regulated end market, potential platform in a fragmented niche, maybe margin expansion opportunity, but growth is only moderate and TAM may be limited.

Corp dev at a larger fintech might say:
Perfect fit because it plugs directly into our banking workflow, shortens sales cycles, creates cross-sell, and removes the need to partner with third parties.

Or the reverse.

Take a data infrastructure business with messy services mix but strong technical product and high customer overlap with an acquirer.

PE might say:
Too messy, poor visibility, implementation-heavy, QoE risk, hard to exit.

Corp dev might say:
Messy standalone, but strategically valuable because it solves a real product gap and can be cleaned up post-acquisition inside the platform.

What each one prioritizes in sourcing

For PE, the most important early signals are often:

recurring revenue quality,
margin profile,
fragmentation,
customer stickiness,
management strength,
scale path,
and realistic entry price.

For corp dev, the most important early signals are often:

same buyer,
same workflow,
product adjacency,
integration feasibility,
cross-sell logic,
and whether the target fills a strategic hole that internal build cannot solve fast enough.

That is the cleanest distinction.

Horizontal vs vertical from PE vs corp dev

Horizontal deals for PE are often about consolidation, density, and multiple arbitrage if the strategy is buy-and-build.

Horizontal deals for corp dev are more about market share, customer base, eliminating competitive pressure, product breadth, or geographic coverage.

Vertical deals for PE can work, but usually only if the value chain logic still supports a coherent platform story and eventual exit narrative. PE does not want a random collection of adjacent assets that only make sense to one odd buyer.

Vertical deals for corp dev are much more natural because strategic buyers can monetize adjacency better. They can bundle, integrate, rationalize, and use internal channels. So corp dev often has more freedom to pursue vertical logic that PE would consider too bespoke.

Differences in deliverables upward

A PE senior associate typically needs to present something closer to an investment case:

why this subsector,
why this target,
what quality signals exist,
how it could underwrite,
what entry range might look like,
what the value-creation levers are,
and what the exit path could be.

A corp dev VP usually needs to present something closer to a strategic action memo:

what gap this target fills,
what synergies exist,
how it fits the roadmap,
what integration issues matter,
what the org impact is,
and whether the strategic value justifies price and complexity.

The tone is different too.

PE materials are usually more skeptical about downside and valuation.
Corp dev materials are usually more detailed on product, commercial overlap, and integration.

How the scorecard changes

If I were scoring targets for PE, I would probably weight:

business quality,
growth durability,
margin / cash profile,
fragmentation and roll-up fit,
management,
valuation realism,
and exitability.

If I were scoring for corp dev, I would weight:

strategic fit,
customer/workflow overlap,
product complementarity,
technical integration,
synergy capture,
organizational feasibility,
and then standalone financial quality after that.

Both still care about ownership and sellability.

One major practical difference: sellability means different things

For PE, sellability means:
Can I get into a bilateral or limited process at a sensible price? Is the founder rational? Is the asset financeable? Is there a clean path to control?

For corp dev, sellability means:
Can I get internal alignment to do this? Will product, engineering, sales, and finance support it? Even if the founder wants to sell, can my company absorb it?

This is important because corp dev often dies internally, while PE more often dies on price or underwriting.

Who is usually more flexible?

PE is often more flexible on strategic adjacency if the financial case is strong.

Corp dev is often more flexible on standalone financial imperfections if the strategic case is strong.

That is the blunt version.

Where sourcing behavior differs in practice

A PE associate will spend relatively more time on:
ownership pattern,
prior transaction signals,
financial proxies,
platform vs add-on logic,
and sponsor/arbitrage precedent.

A corp dev VP will spend relatively more time on:
customer references,
product architecture,
integration map,
competitive overlap,
internal stakeholder fit,
and build-vs-buy logic.

So yes, the sourcing engine is similar, but the filtering logic is not.

The simplest way to summarize it

PE asks:
Would this be a good business to own?

Corp dev asks:
Would this make our business better?

Sometimes the answer is yes to both. Those are the best assets and everyone chases them.
Sometimes it is only yes to one. That is where the buyer universe diverges.

**since both Corp Dev VP and PE Senior Associate might hire M&A Senior Associate for their buy-side deal... should we just keep the approach of the M&A Senior Associate? if we're building a sourcing product, what can be common between all 3 functions?**

Yes for the workflow. No for the decision logic.

If you are building a sourcing product, the safest design choice is:

use the M&A Senior Associate workflow as the operational backbone,
but do not use the M&A Senior Associate’s objective function as the product’s brain.

That distinction matters.

The banker is usually the operator. They define the mandate, build the universe, enrich targets, rank them, and package the output. That operating loop is very similar whether the end client is PE or corp dev.

But the banker is not the principal. The principal is either:
a PE team trying to buy a good asset at a workable price with a credible exit path, or
a corp dev team trying to buy the right asset for strategy and integration.

So if you encode only the banker workflow, you get a good research assistant. If you encode the principal’s decision logic on top of that, you get a real sourcing product.

The right mental model for the product is three layers.

First layer: common sourcing engine.
Second layer: mandate-specific scoring logic.
Third layer: persona-specific outputs and workflow views.

What can be common between all 3 functions

A lot, actually. The common ground is the entire pre-diligence target identification stack.

All three need to do the same basic jobs:

define the acquisition thesis,
translate that into a target pattern,
map the market and value chain,
generate a target universe,
enrich each company with evidence,
prioritize targets,
track outreach and feedback,
and keep an institutional memory of what was reviewed and why.

That is the product core.

In practical terms, the common modules are these.

Mandate capture

All three need a structured way to define:
what the buyer wants,
what is in scope and out of scope,
whether the logic is horizontal, vertical, or adjacency,
target geography,
size range,
business model constraints,
ownership preferences,
and known red flags.

This should be common.

Market map and value-chain mapping

All three need a way to break a sector into:
customer segments,
workflows,
product categories,
value-chain positions,
competitors,
adjacent vendors,
and relevant ecosystem nodes.

This is common and extremely important. It is where good sourcing starts.

Universe generation

All three need the same discovery primitives:
keyword search,
peer expansion,
similar-company discovery,
category clustering,
customer-backward mapping,
workflow-based discovery,
conference/vendor directory mining,
and company database screening.

Common again.

Target enrichment

All three need structured company cards with evidence on:
what the company does,
who buys it,
product modules,
customer proof,
recurring revenue quality,
size proxies,
ownership,
sellability,
growth proxies,
and obvious red flags.

Common.

Ranking infrastructure

All three need a way to score and sort targets. The infrastructure is common even if the weights differ.

Common dimensions usually include:
fit,
quality,
executability,
risk,
and some form of upside.

The mistake is to turn that into one universal score. The infrastructure should be shared; the weighting should not.

Workflow and collaboration

All three need:
notes,
sources,
confidence levels,
next actions,
who reviewed what,
why a company was rejected,
and a history of outreach / prior interactions.

That is pure common plumbing.

Explainability

All three need to understand why a name is ranked highly. Black-box rankings are not acceptable in M&A. A good sourcing product has to show the evidence chain.

This is common and non-negotiable.

What should not be common

The final prioritization logic should not be shared as a single default model.

That is where PE, corp dev, and advisory diverge.

PE will weight standalone quality, entry valuation realism, margin potential, resilience, platform/add-on logic, and exitability more heavily.

Corp dev will weight customer/workflow overlap, product adjacency, technical integration, strategic gap-fill, channel leverage, and synergy capture more heavily.

The M&A Senior Associate at a bank will often optimize for:
mandate fit,
breadth of credible names,
actionability,
and quality of recommendation to the client.
That is useful, but it is not the same thing as principal-side value creation.

So the product should not have one “target attractiveness score.”
It should have a shared fact base and multiple scoring views.

What to use as the design center

Use the M&A Senior Associate as the power user for the workflow.
Use the deal thesis as the design center for the scoring.
Use PE / corp dev as the decision views.

That is the cleanest answer.

Why?

Because the senior associate is usually the person doing the heavy lifting. They are the most frequent user and the one who will live in the product daily. So if you want usage and data quality, build for their operating needs.

But if you optimize the whole product around that person’s default behavior, you risk building a longlist generator instead of a decision tool.

The product should therefore think in terms of mandates, not job titles.

That is the key design choice:
not “PE mode vs corp dev mode vs banker mode,”
but “this mandate is a PE platform thesis,” “this mandate is a PE add-on thesis,” “this mandate is a corp dev horizontal fill-in,” “this mandate is a corp dev vertical workflow expansion,” etc.

That is much more robust than persona-based product design.

What the common data model should look like

If you are building this properly, the core object should not just be a company.

It should be:
company facts
plus
mandate-specific fit assessment.

That is critical.

A company can be a great target for one mandate and irrelevant for another. So the product needs to separate:
stable company attributes
from
thesis-specific interpretation.

Stable company facts are things like:
business description,
products,
customers,
geography,
ownership,
size proxies,
revenue model,
employee count,
funding,
tech/integration clues,
customer logos,
partner ecosystem,
and transaction history.

Mandate-specific assessment is:
why this company fits this buyer,
what the specific synergy or investment logic is,
what risks matter under this thesis,
and how it ranks against other names for this mandate.

If you collapse those two layers, your product gets messy very quickly.

What the common score dimensions should be

I would keep the scoring architecture common and let the weights vary.

The most reusable dimensions are:

Strategic fit.
Does this company match the stated thesis?

Business quality.
Is this a real, defensible, scalable business?

Executability.
Can it realistically transact?

Evidence strength.
How confident are we in what we think we know?

Complexity/risk.
How messy is this likely to be?

Optional upside vectors.
This is where you branch into synergy upside for corp dev or value-creation upside for PE.

Then each function can weight them differently.

For example:
PE might emphasize business quality, executability, and value-creation potential.
Corp dev might emphasize strategic fit, synergy, and integration feasibility.
Advisory might emphasize mandate fit, credibility of rationale, and actionability.

What the product should do for all 3

If I were defining the common feature set, I would include:

A structured mandate builder.
A market-map builder.
A company discovery engine.
A target card with evidence capture.
A ranking and shortlist engine.
A reject log with reasons.
A relationship/outreach tracker.
A recommendation memo generator.
A feedback loop that learns from accepted/rejected names.

Those are common across all 3.

The feedback loop is especially valuable. The product should learn from user behavior:
which names get moved up,
which get killed,
what reasons recur,
what categories are overrepresented,
and which signals correlate with “good target” for a given buyer type.

That is where product defensibility starts to appear.

What should be persona-specific in the UI

The front-end view can and should change by user.

A banker view should emphasize:
universe coverage,
ranking rationale,
presentation quality,
and actionability.

A PE view should emphasize:
platform vs add-on classification,
ownership,
financial proxies,
QoE risk,
exit logic,
and valuation sanity.

A corp dev view should emphasize:
product map,
customer overlap,
integration points,
build-vs-buy logic,
synergy capture,
and internal stakeholder implications.

Same data layer. Different lenses.

That is much better than separate products.

Where the real common denominator sits

The deepest common denominator is this:

all three are trying to reduce search cost and judgment cost under uncertainty.

They all need help answering:
who exists,
which names matter,
why they matter,
and which ones are worth spending time on.

That is the shared product problem.

The divergence happens only after that:
PE asks, “Is this a good asset to own?”
Corp dev asks, “Does this improve our company?”
Banking asks, “Is this a credible and actionable recommendation for the client?”

So yes, you can build one sourcing product for all three.
But it should be one common engine with configurable decision layers, not one rigid workflow copied from advisory.

If I were making the product decision today, I would do this:

Build the MVP around the M&A Senior Associate workflow because that is the most operationally rich and repeatable user behavior.
But architect the scoring, ranking, and outputs around mandate templates, not banker defaults.

That gives you:
high-frequency usage,
shared data quality,
and flexibility across PE and corp dev.

The blunt version is:
keep the banker’s process,
not the banker’s worldview.

core entities,
mandatory fields,
score dimensions,
workflow states,
and PE vs corp dev vs advisor views.
